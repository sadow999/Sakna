My Story

A Question Born
at Dabaa

This portfolio documents how a question asked on a nuclear power plant site in 2022 became a career at the intersection of water science, cultural heritage, and climate resilience. I am an environmental engineer, a water resources scientist, and a UNESCO consultant. Everything you see here flows from one persistent inquiry.

In 2022, I was a Project Engineer at Hassan Allam Construction, managing critical water supply and firefighting infrastructure for 174+ buildings at the El Dabaa Nuclear Power Plant — one of Africa's largest active infrastructure programs, on Egypt's Mediterranean coast. I coordinated with five multinational stakeholder groups, enforced nuclear-grade QA/QC standards, and supervised a concrete batch plant with a capacity of 240 m³/hour.

But every day at the water's edge, working in a basin with deep ecological and human history, questions that had always lived at the edge of my attention came into sharp focus. Who owns the water rights in this basin? What happens to the environmental integrity of the Mediterranean coast as we build here at this scale? These weren't rhetorical questions — they were engineering questions with no good answers in the technical specifications.

"Technical precision without cultural and environmental grounding is not engineering. It is just construction."

Supported by managers who saw the same tensions, I began researching systematically. That inquiry led me from the construction site to the corridors of IHE Delft in the Netherlands — where I earned an MSc applying machine learning to satellite-derived water consumption data across the Nile Delta. And then to UNESCO Cairo, where I found the language, the institutional mandate, and the community to build the practice I had been moving toward.

Today I work at the intersection of machine learning and hydrology, cultural heritage and climate policy, scientific rigor and indigenous knowledge. The thread connecting everything — El Dabaa, IHE Delft, UNESCO — is the conviction that water is not a resource to be managed. It is a civilizational thread to be understood.

Desert · Oasis Biomes · Western Egypt · MAB Programme

Restoration of Historic Oases

Combatting Climate Change & Advancing the Green Economy in Egypt's Western Desert · 2026–2028
€535,000 24 Months

The Situation: Our Forensic Agrarian Review during the inception phase documented a "Phenological Shift" — climate change is physically altering the seasonal cycles and harvest dates of staple crops like dates and olives, throwing traditional farming into chaos. Fossil aquifers are depleting at rates exceeding recharge by factors of 3–5x. The ancestral "Knowledge by Sight" — the intricate unwritten methods of managing scarce water that have sustained these oases for millennia — is dying with the elder generation. If we do not act immediately to bridge ancient heritage with modern science, these green havens will be consumed by the desert.

Four site-specific pilots — each addressing a distinct challenge
Dakhla Oasis
Water Harvesting

The ancient Nubian Sandstone Aquifer is fading, drying up artesian channels that once flowed freely. UNESCO's IHP principles applied: 2–3 traditional wells rehabilitated, 2 sand dams + recharge trench constructed to capture flash flood runoff, 3ha of gravity-fed drip irrigation installed — eliminating the need for diesel pumps.

Fayoum Oasis
Agro-Innovation

The Ramad Farm Model deployed across 10ha: ancient "Fallow Irrigation" revived alongside intercropping of date palms with legumes, moringa, and henna. Crucially, university-developed bio-polymers dramatically increase soil water retention — a bridge between 4,000-year-old farming technique and 21st-century materials science.

Siwa Oasis
Salinity Management

Rising salty groundwater is seeping into fields, poisoning the famous Siwan olives and date crops, and threatening the ancient mud-brick Shali heritage site. Bioengineering response: 8ha of highly salt-tolerant heritage crops, 150 agroforestry windbreak trees to stabilize soil structure, 2 wetland strips for natural water filtration and ecosystem recovery.

Kharga Oasis
Eco-Tourism & TVET

The absence of green economic opportunities drives youth migration, accelerating the loss of traditional knowledge. A historic 1960s Administrative Complex is restored as a School of Traditional Crafts. A mudbrick house is converted to a visitor centre. Local youth are trained as heritage geo-tourism guides, creating livelihoods tied to cultural stewardship.

The Genetic Ark: Through our partnership with the Desert Research Center (DRC), we identified a dormant Tissue Culture Laboratory during the inception phase. Operational funds are provided to reactivate it — and 50,000 seedlings of endangered, climate-resilient heritage date varieties (Saidi and Siwi) will be mass-propagated bio-securely, securing the genetic future of Egypt's oases against climate shocks. Additionally, 20 women-led startups are trained to convert agricultural waste — date pits, coffee grounds, olive pomace — into organic flour, animal feed, and natural cosmetics: a self-sustaining circular bio-economy that breaks the grant-dependency cycle.

Coastal · Blue Carbon · Red Sea · IOC Ocean Literacy

Green Economy & Mangroves

Strengthening Coastal Resilience & Blue Carbon in Egypt's Red Sea · Wadi El Gemal & Hamata · 2026–2028
€500,000 24 Months

The Situation: A devastating 30% decline in Red Sea mangrove cover, driven by rapid urbanization, unregulated camel overgrazing, and climate change. But our inception review identified a deeper problem: Past Project Failure. Previous international restoration initiatives often failed because they relied on guesswork — planting seedlings in wrong sediment depths, ignoring local hydrology, and excluding the communities who hold 20 years of unpublished ecological knowledge. We must stop treating mangroves as 'nature to be conserved' and start treating them as the foundation of a thriving Blue Carbon Economy.

Four scientifically validated, shovel-ready pillars
Pillar 1
Precision Ecological Restoration

Restore 20km of coastline by planting 20,000 mangrove trees (10,000/year) across 2 local nurseries. Planting zones are dictated by 40 years of Egyptian Space Agency (EgSA) satellite survival baselines (1985–2025) combined with Ranger field logs on optimal salinity levels — eliminating guesswork.

Pillar 2
Protection Infrastructure

Install camel-proof fencing around every planting zone to prevent overgrazing of young shoots before they can anchor. Physically restore degraded tidal channels to reinstate correct hydrological flow — the critical condition for root establishment that past projects ignored.

Pillar 3
Green Skills & TVET

Formal vocational training for 60 local youth and women in sustainable fisheries management, mangrove nursery operations, and eco-tourism guiding. This certifies them as economic guardians of the ecosystem — their income now depends on the forest's health, not its exploitation.

Pillar 4
Scientific Monitoring

Continuous NDVI Vegetation Index analysis via EgSA satellite imagery tracks forest health in near-real-time. The project culminates in a peer-reviewed Mangrove Restoration Model, submitted to national policymakers to advise future coastal restoration decisions across Egypt.

The 40-Year Satellite Baseline & The Ababda Bedouin: Through our EgSA partnership, we conducted a retrospective remote sensing analysis of Earth Observation data from 1985 to 2025 — identifying exactly where mangroves historically thrived before urban encroachment. This allows planting funds to be directed with surgical precision. The Ababda Bedouin Tribes — indigenous custodians of the Wadi El Gemal coastline — are institutionalized as the central stewards of the Community Mangrove Stewardship Committee (CMSC), alongside the Wadi El Gemal National Park Authority, EgSA, AUC (CARES), NIOF, and HEPCA. Their economic survival is now directly tied to the health of the mangrove bio-shields.

Intellectual Framework · Arab MAB Programme · Publications

Drylands, Oases &
Heritage-Based Solutions

The publications and policy work at UNESCO Cairo are grounded in a single paradigm shift that the AECID projects operationalize: oases are not merely natural systems requiring technical interventions. They are heritage-based solutions — socio-ecological systems where cultural practices, traditional knowledge, and social structures are as integral to ecological function as hydrology itself.

The Core Empirical Finding

Linguistic diversity in oasis communities directly correlates with agrobiodiversity. The Siwi Amazigh language contains precise terminology for traditional cultivation techniques and water management practices that have maintained date palm genetic diversity for centuries. When the language erodes, the knowledge erodes. And when the knowledge erodes, the ecosystem erodes. The loss of traditional knowledge is as direct a threat to oasis sustainability as aquifer depletion — and far faster.

This framework was developed through three field missions: to Fayoum University's Faculty of Agriculture (where significant research capacity exists but a critical gap remains between academic findings and farmer practice — extension services are underdeveloped, and interdisciplinary integration between hydrology, ecology, agriculture, and social science is absent); to Siwa Oasis (where a technical intervention in the 1990s successfully reduced waterlogging — but governance failure allowed illegal drilling to resume and the problem to re-emerge); and to New Valley Governorate (where the Nubian Sandstone Aquifer is being extracted at rates far exceeding recharge, and infrastructure deficits force communities into resource-intensive survival strategies).

These findings were validated and expanded through the 12-country Expert Group Meeting on Managing Groundwater and Dependent Ecosystems in Drylands, hosted at UNESCO Cairo in June 2025 — producing the strategic framework now being implemented across the AECID projects.

Siwa, Egypt

Technical success + governance failure = system collapse. Waterlogging returned when enforcement and community engagement ended.

Azraq, Jordan

Aflaj Systems, Oman

Draa Valley, Morocco

Strong legal framework + community "groundwater contracts" + smart metering = measurable improvement in aquifer management.

New Valley, Egypt

Peer-Reviewed Research · MSc Thesis · IHE Delft 2024

The Zankalon Forecast:
When ML Meets the Nile Delta

My MSc thesis produced a counterintuitive finding that challenges standard assumptions about how climate warming affects crop water demand — and revealed that national land-use policy, not climate change itself, is the dominant driver of Egypt's future agricultural water crisis.

MSc Thesis · Under Peer Review · 2026 · IHE Delft

"Assessing the Impact of Climate Change on Agricultural Water Consumption Using Remote Sensing Data and Machine Learning: A Case Study of Zankalon, Nile Delta, Egypt"

🛰

Data: WaPOR v2 (30m resolution AETI data, 2009–2023), AgERA5 climate reanalysis drivers, ensemble of 23 CMIP6 General Circulation Models. Six major irrigated crops: wheat, rice, clover, grapes, orchards, potato. Projections extended to 2099 under SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5.

🤖

Method: Nine ML algorithm types evaluated per crop using time-series cross-validation. Best performers: Random Forest (Orchards R²=0.863, Wheat R²=0.684), SVR (Rice R²=0.832, Grapes R²=0.677, Potato R²=0.766), Ridge Regression (Clover R²=0.770). No single algorithm dominated across all crops.

❄️

Counter-intuitive finding 1: Winter crops (wheat, clover) show INCREASING water demand (+5.3–6.6% by 2099). Warmer winters increase evaporative demand during the cool growing season — hotter summers do not automatically mean all crops need more water.

🌊

Counter-intuitive finding 2: Summer crops (rice, grapes) show DECREASING water demand (−4.7% to −6.3%). Likely driven by projected changes in humidity and radiation balance during peak summer months, reducing vapour pressure deficit despite rising temperatures.

📊

Policy finding: Agricultural expansion will drive a +13.5% total CWR increase by 2050 — vs. +3.6% from climate change alone. National land-use policy is the critical lever. The crops most targeted for expansion (wheat, orchards) are precisely those projected to need the most water.

Urban Heritage Framework · UNESCO / Ministry of Tourism & Antiquities

University Heritage Forum:
Historic Cairo as Living Lab

The UHF is a pioneering initiative launched July 2023, bridging 8 universities, the Ministry of Tourism & Antiquities, and Cairo Governorate to develop interdisciplinary conservation strategies for Historic Cairo — a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979. Over 85 participants produced 7 technical reports directly informing the Conservation & Management Plan. My role: GIS map production, technical documentation, and programme coordination. Hover on each theme card to read the detailed output.

Theme 01

Zero-Energy Cities — Citadel Area

Digital Twin of the Mohamed Ali Mosque using Autodesk Revit and Unreal Engine. Enables predictive maintenance scheduling and solar energy integration simulations — a scalable proof-of-concept for heritage buildings as active energy participants rather than passive energy drains.

Output: A replicable Zero-Energy Heritage model with direct policy implications for Cairo Governorate's energy transition planning. Simulation data shared with MoTA for the Conservation and Management Plan.
Theme 02

Green Infrastructure — Fustat

Proposals for urban agriculture, water-sensitive design, rooftop gardens, and reintroduction of historically relevant plant species through participatory landscape planning. Fustat affirmed as a site of agricultural significance for over a millennium — deserving landscape strategies of commensurate depth and ambition.

Output: A community-managed green space framework that restores Fustat's agricultural heritage identity while delivering measurable urban ecosystem services including stormwater management and microclimate regulation.
Theme 03

Cultural Economy — El-Gamaliya

Immersive heritage trails, creative economy clusters, and a proposed visitor centre in Muhib El-Din Hall. Strategic linking of traditional craft industries — copper, glass, leather, textiles, papyrus — with cultural tourism to create sustainable livelihoods embedded within the historic urban fabric.

Output: Heritage Economic Activation Plan converting intangible cultural assets into sustainable income for El-Gamaliya artisan communities, with specific recommendations for the Heritage Investment Plan.
Theme 04

Urban Finance — Darb Al-Ahmar

Adaptive reuse strategies for Khayer Bek (dark tourism VR experiences + artisan market) and Bayt Al-Razzaz (boutique hotel + seminar venue). Innovative financing mechanisms proposed: microfinance schemes, heritage bonds, and eco-transport solutions designed to bridge the gap between conservation costs and private investment appetite.

Output: Creative Financing Toolkit for Heritage Adaptive Reuse — a template applicable across Historic Cairo and potentially scalable to other Egyptian WHS contexts.
Theme 05

Documentation — Eastern Historic Cairo

Meticulous documentation of 25+ monuments in the Mamluk Cemetery using a quantitative risk-based prioritization model assigning urgency scores across structural, environmental, and social risk dimensions. Cemeteries reframed as "living landscapes" with active ecological and social functions far beyond their funerary designation.

Output: Risk-Based Preservation Index for Mamluk Cemetery monuments. A replicable, transparent methodology for preservation policy applicable to other heritage sites across Egypt.
Theme 06

Climate Hazards — Imam Al-Shafi'i Area

Comprehensive climate hazard mapping for one of Cairo's most complex heritage zones. Groundwater management protocols, early-warning systems for flooding and subsidence, and social well-being indicators embedded alongside monument protection targets — ensuring community resilience and conservation are treated as a single integrated objective.

Output: Integrated Climate-Heritage Adaptation Plan with quantified risk thresholds, early-warning triggers, and community response protocols — submitted as input to the CMP.
Theme 07

Living Heritage — Al-Azhar

Crafts, storytelling, and Mawlid practices comprehensively mapped and georeferenced. A Community Management Plan rooted in participatory governance proposes repurposing Sabil-Kuttabs as cultural hubs, anchoring the intangible in the physical urban grid — and ensuring the CMP reflects the living social reality of the district.

Output: "Map of Intangible Heritage" layering active social practices onto Al-Azhar's physical urban fabric. Community Governance Council framework submitted for CMP integration.
