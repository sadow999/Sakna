The Story Begins

A Question Born at Dabaa

This is my portfolio — a record of how a question asked on a nuclear power plant site in 2022 became a career at the intersection of water science, cultural heritage, and climate resilience. I am an environmental engineer. I work at UNESCO. And I believe the Arab world's ancient water wisdom is the most underused resource in global climate adaptation.

In 2022, I was a Project Engineer on one of Africa's largest active infrastructure programs — the El Dabaa Nuclear Power Plant on Egypt's Mediterranean coast. Managing water supply and firefighting networks for 174+ buildings, coordinating with multinational stakeholders, enforcing nuclear QA/QC standards.

But something else was happening. Every day, working next to the sea, in a basin with deep ecological and human history, the questions that had always lived at the edge of my attention came into sharp focus: who owns these water rights? What happens to the environmental integrity of this basin?

Supported by managers who saw the same tensions, I began researching. That inquiry led me from construction sites to IHE Delft — and eventually to UNESCO, where I found the language, the community, and the mandate to pursue what had been quietly building: the intersection of technical rigor and cultural stewardship of water.

Desert · Oasis Biomes · Western Egypt

Restoration of Historic Oases

Combatting Climate Change & Advancing the Green Economy · 2026–2028
€535,000 · 24 Months

Our Forensic Agrarian Review documented a critical "Phenological Shift" — climate change physically altering seasonal cycles of dates and olives. If we do not act immediately to bridge ancient heritage with modern science, these green havens will be consumed by the desert.

Four site-specific pilots: Dakhla (rehabilitate 2–3 traditional wells, sand dams, gravity-fed irrigation), Fayoum (Ramad Farm Model — Fallow Irrigation + bio-polymers across 10ha), Siwa (8ha salt-tolerant heritage crops, 150 agroforestry windbreak trees, 2 wetland strips), Kharga (restore 1960s complex as School of Traditional Crafts, heritage geo-tourism).

Coastal · Blue Carbon · Red Sea

Green Economy & Mangroves

Strengthening Coastal Resilience & Blue Carbon · Wadi El Gemal & Hamata · 2026–2028
€500,000 · 24 Months

A devastating 30% decline in mangrove cover driven by urbanization, camel overgrazing, and climate change. Past initiatives failed by planting in wrong sediment depths or ignoring local hydrology. We must stop treating mangroves as 'nature to be conserved' and start treating them as the foundation of a thriving Blue Carbon Economy.

By merging 40 years of Egyptian Space Agency satellite data (1985–2025) with the Traditional Ecological Knowledge of the Ababda Bedouin Tribes — we direct planting with surgical precision. Four pillars: Precision Ecological Restoration (20,000 trees, 2 nurseries, 20km coastline), Protection Infrastructure (camel-proof fencing, tidal channel restoration), Green Skills TVET for 60 local youth and women, and Continuous NDVI Satellite Monitoring.

Community Mangrove Stewardship Committee (CMSC) established linking Wadi El Gemal National Park Authority, EgSA, AUC (CARES), NIOF, HEPCA, and Ababda Bedouin communities. Economic survival now directly tied to forest health.

Publications & Policy Work · Arab MAB Programme

Drylands, Oases &
Heritage-Based Solutions

A series of publications on Man and the Biosphere and Drylands in the Arab World establishes a paradigm shift: oases are not merely natural systems requiring technical interventions. They are heritage-based solutions where cultural practices, traditional knowledge, and social structures are integral to ecological resilience.

The core insight: linguistic diversity in oasis communities directly correlates with agrobiodiversity. The Siwi Amazigh language contains precise terminology for traditional cultivation and water management that has maintained date palm genetic diversity for centuries. When the language erodes, the ecosystem erodes.

This intellectual framework — developed through field missions to Fayoum University, Siwa Oasis, and New Valley Governorate — underpins the AECID proposals, the WEFE competition, and the 12-country Expert Group meeting on dryland groundwater management (UNESCO Cairo, June 2025).

Urban Heritage Framework · UNESCO / MoTA · Historic Cairo

University Heritage Forum:
A Living Laboratory

Launched July 2023, the UHF is a pioneering initiative positioned as a "living laboratory" for Historic Cairo — a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979. Over 85 participants from 8 universities and 3 public institutions produced 7 detailed technical reports, directly informing Historic Cairo's Conservation and Management Plan in alignment with UNESCO's Historic Urban Landscape Recommendation and Egypt Vision 2030.

Theme 01

Zero-Energy Cities — Citadel Area

A cutting-edge Digital Twin of the Mohamed Ali Mosque was developed using Autodesk Revit and Unreal Engine, enabling predictive maintenance and solar energy simulations to demonstrate the scalability of renewable energy in heritage contexts.

Key Output: A replicable "Zero-Energy City" model for heritage sites in dense urban contexts, with direct policy implications for Cairo Governorate energy planning.
Theme 02

Green Infrastructure — Fustat

Proposals for urban agriculture, water-sensitive design, rooftop gardens, and reintroduction of historically relevant plant species through participatory landscape planning. Fustat was affirmed as part of "the human agriculture project that has been for over a millennium."

Key Output: A community-managed green space framework that restores Fustat's agricultural heritage while delivering measurable ecosystem services to urban residents.
Theme 03

Cultural Economy — El-Gamaliya

Immersive heritage trails, creative economy clusters, and a proposed visitor center in Muhib El-Din Hall. Strategic linking of traditional crafts — copper, glass, textiles — with cultural tourism to create sustainable livelihoods within the historic fabric.

Key Output: A Heritage Economic Activation Plan converting intangible cultural assets into sustainable income streams for artisan communities in El-Gamaliya.
Theme 04

Urban Finance & Adaptive Reuse — Darb Al-Ahmar

Strategies for Khayer Bek (dark tourism VR experiences and artisan market) and Bayt Al-Razzaz (boutique hotel and seminar venue). Innovative financing mechanisms including microfinance schemes, heritage investment plans, and eco-friendly transport proposed.

Key Output: A Creative Financing Toolkit bridging private sector investment with heritage conservation, including a Heritage Investment Plan aligned with Egypt Vision 2030.
Theme 05

Documentation & Conservation — Eastern Cairo

Meticulous documentation of 25+ monuments in the Mamluk Cemetery using a quantitative risk-based prioritization model. Cemeteries redefined as "living landscapes" — critical public spaces with social functions far beyond their funerary purpose.

Key Output: A Risk-Based Preservation Index for Mamluk Cemetery monuments, providing a replicable methodology for other heritage sites across Egypt.
Theme 06

Climate Hazards — Imam Al-Shafi'i Area

Research focused on climate hazard mapping leading to heritage-led adaptation strategies: groundwater management protocols, early-warning systems, and social well-being indicators alongside monument protection — embedding community resilience in conservation plans.

Key Output: An Integrated Climate-Heritage Adaptation Plan with early-warning systems and groundwater management protocols for the Imam Al-Shafi'i district.
Theme 07

Living Heritage & Community — Al-Azhar

Comprehensive mapping of crafts, storytelling, and Mawlid practices. A Community Management Plan rooted in participatory governance and local demands — including repurposing Sabil-Kuttabs as cultural hubs — anchors the intangible in the physical urban grid.

Key Output: A "Map of Intangible Heritage" layering social practices onto Al-Azhar's physical urban grid, with a Community Governance Council framework for the CMP.

Research & Publications

The Zankalon Forecast:
ML & The Nile Delta

MSc Thesis — Under Review for Publication · 2026

"Assessing the Impact of Climate Change on Agricultural Water Consumption Using Remote Sensing Data and Machine Learning: A Case Study of Zankalon, Nile Delta, Egypt"

🛰

Data: WaPOR v2 (30m AETI), AgERA5 reanalysis, 23 CMIP6 GCMs. SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5 through 2099. Six major crops: wheat, rice, clover, grapes, orchards, potato.

❄️

Winter crops (wheat, clover): INCREASING water demand +5.3–6.6% by 2099 under SSP5-8.5, driven by warmer cool-season conditions increasing evaporative demand.

🌊

Summer crops (rice, grapes): DECREASING water demand −4.7% to −6.3% — counter-intuitive result driven by projected humidity and radiation changes during peak summer months.

📊

Policy finding: Agricultural expansion will drive +13.5% CWR increase by 2050 — far exceeding the 3.6% from climate change alone. Land-use policy is the critical lever for water security.

🤖

Models: Random Forest (orchards R²=0.863, wheat R²=0.684), SVR (rice R²=0.832, potato R²=0.766), Ridge Regression (clover R²=0.770).

Projected Crop Impacts (SSP5-8.5 · 2099)

Wheat (Winter)

+6.6%

Warmer winters increase evaporative demand in the cool growing season.

Clover (Winter)

+6.6%

Same warming trend across the winter growing season.

Orchards

+4.2%

Year-round integration of seasonal shifts shows moderate net increase.

Rice (Summer)

-6.3%

Counter-intuitive decrease: humidity/radiation changes reduce vapour pressure deficit.

Grapes (Summer)

-4.0%

Reduced atmospheric demand despite rising temperatures.

Potato

+0.4%

Near-neutral: summer and nili season effects largely cancel out.

Policy Implication: The crops most likely to benefit from national expansion plans (wheat, orchards) are precisely those projected to require more water under future climate conditions — making high-efficiency irrigation investments critical for the viability of those expansion plans.
